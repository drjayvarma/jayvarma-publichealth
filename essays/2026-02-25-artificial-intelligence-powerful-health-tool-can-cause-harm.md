# AI might predict suicide risk — but that doesn’t mean we should use it

This week, I’m writing about a provocative study suggesting that artificial intelligence can identify people at risk of suicide before they even show symptoms.

## Can AI predict suicide?

A study published last week in the journal *Nature Medicine* claims that artificial intelligence can identify people at high risk of suicide years before they attempt to take their own lives. The researchers trained a machine-learning model on data from more than 6 million adults in Denmark, including health care records, social services data, and demographic information.

The system was designed to analyze hundreds of variables — such as diagnoses, prescriptions, employment status, and income — to detect patterns that might signal elevated suicide risk.

According to the authors, the model correctly identified about 50% of people who later attempted suicide, while labeling only about 5% of the overall population as high risk.

Those results are striking. Suicide prevention has long struggled with the challenge of predicting who will attempt suicide. Most people who experience depression, trauma, or other mental health conditions never attempt suicide, while some people who die by suicide have never been diagnosed with a mental illness.

If AI could reliably identify people at high risk before they show obvious symptoms, it could theoretically allow health systems to intervene earlier — offering counseling, treatment, or social support.

## The problem of prediction without action

Despite the excitement surrounding this research, we should be cautious about rushing to implement predictive AI systems for suicide risk.

First, prediction alone does not save lives. Identifying someone as “high risk” only helps if there are effective, acceptable interventions available and systems capable of delivering them.

Even when people are already known to be at high risk — for example, after a previous suicide attempt or hospitalization for severe depression — health systems often struggle to provide consistent follow-up care.

Adding millions of people flagged by an algorithm could easily overwhelm already strained mental health services.

Second, predictive models inevitably generate false positives. In this study, about 5% of the population would be labeled high risk even though the vast majority of those individuals would never attempt suicide.

Being incorrectly labeled as high risk could have real consequences for people’s lives, potentially affecting employment, insurance, or how they are treated by institutions.

Third, the model relies on extensive personal data, including sensitive information from health care, social services, and government records. Deploying such systems raises profound questions about privacy, consent, and surveillance.

## The broader ethical challenge

Predictive algorithms are becoming increasingly common in medicine, criminal justice, education, and finance. In many cases, these systems promise to identify risks earlier and more accurately than humans can.

But prediction is not the same as prevention.

In suicide prevention, the most effective interventions are often relatively simple: ensuring access to mental health care, reducing access to lethal means, and strengthening social support networks.

These strategies save lives without requiring massive surveillance systems or complex algorithms.

Before embracing AI tools that attempt to predict human behavior, we should ask a basic question: Are we investing in technology because it is the best solution, or because it is the most technologically impressive one?
