# The quest for longevity biomarkers continues — but the gains may be modest

This week, I’m writing about a new study that aims to predict how long people will live — using thousands of proteins measured in their blood.

## A blood test to predict lifespan?

Researchers recently published a study describing a new method for predicting mortality risk based on patterns of proteins circulating in the bloodstream. The study analyzed blood samples from more than 50,000 people in the United Kingdom, measuring roughly 3,000 different proteins.

Using machine learning techniques, the researchers identified a subset of proteins that were strongly associated with future risk of death. They then used those proteins to build a model that could estimate a person’s likelihood of dying within the next 5 to 10 years.

The idea behind this approach is straightforward. Proteins in the bloodstream reflect biological processes occurring throughout the body, including inflammation, metabolism, immune function, and tissue damage. Changes in these proteins may signal the early stages of disease long before symptoms appear.

In theory, a blood-based “proteomic clock” could allow doctors to identify people whose biological aging is progressing faster than expected and intervene earlier.

## How much can prediction actually change?

While the findings are scientifically interesting, the real-world impact may be more limited than headlines suggest.

The study reported that the protein-based model could explain a substantial portion of variation in mortality risk across individuals. But many of the strongest predictors were proteins already linked to well-known diseases such as heart disease, cancer, kidney disease, and chronic inflammation.

In other words, the model may largely be detecting early signs of diseases that physicians already know how to diagnose and manage using conventional tests.

More importantly, predicting mortality risk is not the same as preventing death. Even if doctors know someone has a higher likelihood of dying in the next decade, it is not always clear what interventions would meaningfully change that trajectory.

For many chronic diseases, the most effective strategies remain familiar ones: controlling blood pressure, managing diabetes, avoiding tobacco, maintaining a healthy weight, exercising regularly, and receiving recommended preventive care.

## The limits of precision longevity

The broader field of longevity science is increasingly focused on identifying biomarkers — measurable biological signals that reflect aging or disease risk.

Companies are now offering consumers a wide array of tests that promise to measure biological age using DNA methylation patterns, blood proteins, metabolites, and other markers.

These technologies are scientifically fascinating, but we should be cautious about assuming they will dramatically extend human life expectancy.

Over the past century, the biggest gains in life expectancy came from improvements in sanitation, vaccination, safer workplaces, and better treatment of infectious diseases. More recent gains have come from controlling cardiovascular risk factors and improving cancer treatment.

By comparison, the additional benefit from fine-tuning risk prediction among people who already have access to modern health care may be relatively modest.

The search for biomarkers of aging will continue to produce important scientific insights. But if our goal is to help people live longer and healthier lives, the most powerful tools we already have may still be the simplest ones.
